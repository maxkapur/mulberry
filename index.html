How should I determine my utility values?

College preferability isn‚Äôt exactly a linear thing‚Äîsome schools might offer a better student experience, whereas others might offer better job prospects. It‚Äôs up to you to decide how to trade off these various factors.

Utility is an economic concept that involves assigning numbers to how much you value things. Even though people can‚Äôt always say what price they place on something, sometimes they reveal it by, for example, canceling their Netflix subscription after a certain price increase.

One way to get a utility estimate for the schools you are considering is by answering the following question: If a letter of admission to this college went up for auction, how much money would I be willing to pay for it? Then divide your answer by some constant so you can fit it in the scale above.

How does it work?

Your goal, in applying to college, is (probably) to maximize the utility of the best school you get into. If you apply to just one school, then your expected utility is the admissions probability times the utility value. But if you apply to many schools, your expected utility is a conditional expression that depends on whether you get into your first choice, your second choice, and so on.

It turns out that the optimal application strategy, under these assumptions, can be characterized by a permutation of the schools. If you can only apply to one college, you should apply to the first school in that permutation; if you can apply to two colleges, you should apply to the first two, and so on.

Mulberry takes your admissions probabilities and utility evaluations and outputs this permutation. The underlying algorithm is described in my MS thesis.
