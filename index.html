How does the algorithm work?

Your goal, in applying to college, is (probably) to maximize the utility of the best school you get into. If you apply to just one school, then your expected utility is the admissions probability times the utility value. But if you apply to many schools, your expected utility is a conditional expression that depends on whether you get into your first choice, your second choice, and so on.

It turns out that the optimal application strategy can be characterized by a certain permutation (ordering) of the schools. If you can only apply to one college, you should apply to the first school in that permutation; if you can apply to two colleges, you should apply to the first two, and so on. Mulberry takes your admissions probabilities and utility parameters and computes this permutation.

How do I determine my utility values?

Utility is an economic concept that involves assigning numbers to how much you value things. Even though people can‚Äôt always say what price they place on something, sometimes they reveal it by, for example, canceling their Netflix subscription after a price increase.

College preferability is multidimensional: Some schools offer an exciting student experience; others, better job prospects. Everyone views the tradeoff among these factors differently. But the utility value is meant to summarize, in a single number, how much you like each school in light of your own priorities and values.

A good way to estimate the utility of the schools you are considering is to ask yourself the following question: If letters of admission to this college were sold at 7/11, how much money would I be willing to pay for one? Then divide your answer by some constant so you can fit it in the scale above.
